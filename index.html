Project Overview

A focused story about immigration, employment, and unequal outcomes in Iceland

This project examines the impact of immigration on Iceland between 2011 and 2021, geographically and labour-market wise. It is the result of a broader exploratory analysis conducted on the population statistics of Iceland within this period, across background, education, and labour thematic areas.

Target audience

Policy and government readers interested in demographic change, workforce sustainability, and labour-market integration.

What the visualizations do

Chapter 1 | The Shift

The choropleth map highlights the immigrant share byIceland's municipalities and the grouped bar chart shows the change in population disaggregated by age group, both among immigrants and natives.

Chapter 2 | Employment Reversal

The slope charts shows immigrant employment rates moved from being ahead of natives' as of 2011 and behind by 2021. The dot plot further breaks that reversal down by age group, with an education level for both charts.

Chapter 3 | Qualification Paradox

The heatmap shows the employment-rate between between immigrants and natives across the different age groups and education levels, highlighting where inequality occurs the most.

How the interactions work

Chapter 1 features a bi-directional interaction between the Choropleth and Grouped Bar Charts.
Clicking a municipality on the map filters the grouped bar chart to show figures for that location, while clicking a bar on the grouped bar chart highlights the five municipalities where that age-group trend is strongest.

Chapter 2 features a uni-directional interaction between the Slope and Horizontal Dot Plot Charts.
The education-level filters update the data in both charts, showing the outcomes in the selected level.

Design decisions

Following feedback from the Design phase, the project was narrowed from several findings during the EDA to one central theme: how immigration changed Iceland's population structure and how that shift connects to labour-market ineqaulity.

The intial design considered three audiences. The final version focuses on a policy and government audience to ensure the narrative and interaction designs remain more purposeful.

Limitations and Future Work

Current Limitation

Attempts to test if native employment advantages are concentrated in particular industries proved futile as the available datasets on Statistics Iceland do not break down employment by sector and background.

Future Improvement

Future improves would focus on extending the interactivity and usability of the story.
Possible next steps include: linking Chapter 2 and 3 more directly, adding clearer active-filter and reset controls, zoom functionality for the choropleth map, and improving accessibility through keyboard navigation, stronger focus states, and a more mobile-responsibe interface.

Chapter 01 | Population Shift

Immigration is reshaping Iceland - geopgraphically and demographically

In 2011, most municipalities had immigrant shares below 8%. By 2021, municipalities in the capital area and southwest stood out with much higher shares, in some cases approaching 30%.

This growth was concentrated in working-age groups, while native population change was much lower and some age bands negative. The pattern is consistent with the 2008 - 2011 Icelandic Financial post-crisis recovery Wikipedia 2008-2011 Icelandic Financial Crisis Iceland's banking collapse triggered a deep recession, followed by recovery driven by exports, tourism, and renewed labour demand. Chapter 02 | Employment reversal

In 2011, immigrants were more likely to be employed. By 2021, the reverse had become true.

In 2011, immigrants were 7.6pp more likely to be employed than natives. BY 2021, they were 2.66 pp less likely to be employed, marking a full reversal over the decade.

The slope chart shows this broad shift clearly, while the horizontal dot plot reveals that the reversal is not evenly distributed across age groups. In 2021, the employment gap is widest across prime working ages, showing that the decline is not limited to a specific part of the labour force. Once filtered by education, the pattern becomes even more pronounced: among those with teriary education, immigrants fall 16.9 percentage points behind equally qualified natives. Rather than closing the gap, higher education is associated with one of the sharpest disparities in the labour market.

When filtered to tertiary education, the gap widens to -16.9pp, meaning equally qualified immigrants are still far less likely to be employed than natives. This suggests that the problem is not simply one of education, but of how education is translated into labour-market opportunity.

Chapter 03 | Qualification Paradox

Equal degrees, unequal outcomes

At lower education levels, the employment gap is smaller and in some cases non-existent. Among those with basic education, immigrants are close to parity in most age groups and exceed natives in some cases. But the pattern reverses sharply as qualifications rise At upper-secondary level, immigrants fall behind across most age groups, and at tertiary level, the disadvantage becomes both larger and more consistent.

The heatmap shows that tertiary-educated immigrants are behind equally qualified natives in every working-age group, with the gap reaching -24.8pp for ages 25-34 and remaining above -19pp through ages 55-64. This suggests that the problem is not a lack of education, but the barriers that prevent qualifications from translating into equal labour-market outcomes.

For tertiary-educated immigrants, the employment gap is negative in every working-age group, reaching -24.8pp among those aged 25-34.

Policy Implications

Iceland is importing talent it is not fully using

Immigration has helped stabilise Iceland's workforce, but persistent barriers continue to limit how fully that talent is used. If left unaddressed, these barriers could undermine the long-term benefits of demographic renewal. The data highlights where policy action is most needed.

01
Reform qualification recognition
The -24.8pp gap among tertiary-educated people aged 25-34 is the clearest sign that formal qualifications are not being translated into equal labour-market outcomes. This should be the highest-priority integration issue.
02
Provide targeted professional integration support
Closing the gap will require more than education alone. Policy should focus on language support, professional networks, and sector-specific entry pathways for highly educated immigrants.
03
Early intervention
The employment gap appears at ages 16-24 and persists across the working-age population. Earlier support could prevent temporary disadvantage from becoming long-term exclusion.
