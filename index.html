Between 2011 and 2021 Iceland's immigrant population more than doubled, with immigrants arriving young, educated, and willing to work. But a decade later, a university-educated immigrant is 16.9 percentage points (pp) less likely to be employed than an equally qualified Icelander. This data story presents findings from an explanatory data analysis on Iceland's population shift from 2011 to 2021.

Chapter 01 | Population Shift

Immigration is reshaping Iceland - geopgraphically and demographically

In 2011 most municipalities had an immigrant share below 8%. By 2021 the capital region and southwest show shares approaching 30%.

The working class group accounted for the highest number of immigrants within this period. This can be linked to the economic recovery in the aftermath of the 2008 - 2011 Icelandic Financial Crisis, which resulted in a renewed inflow of migrants, largely motivated by labour market demand. Chapter 02 | Employment collapse

In 2021, immigrants were better employed. By 2021, reverse became the case.

In 2011, immigrants were 7.6pp ahead of natives in employment. By 2021, they had fallen 2.66pp behind: a complete reversal across ever age group and education level simultaneously.

While this is not a problem in itself, a deeper dive into the employment rates by education level, highlights a varying discrepancy between immigrants and natives.

When filtered to tertiary education, the immigrants employment gap widens to -16.9pp behind equally qualified natives, exonerating education barrier as a possible cause for this disparity.

Chapter 03 | Qualification Paradox

Equal degrees, unequal outcomes

At basic education level, immigrants outperform natives by 11.3pp, indicating that they take any available work. However, at university level, an equally qualified immigrant is 16.9pp less likely to be employed. This is the case at every age group without exception.

Policy Implications

Iceland is importing talent it is not fully using

While the dependency ratio is stable today, the long-term cost of the structural barriers facing tertiary-educated immigrations would exceed the short-term demographic benefit, if not addressed. The data pinpoints where action is needed.
